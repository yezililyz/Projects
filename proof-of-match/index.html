Proof of Match – AI-powered deal-ready matching for VC & Web3 events

01 · Overview

Overview

High-intent, small-room event organizers in the Web3 and venture capital space bring capital, infrastructure, and policy decision makers together for deal-ready conversations. Today’s tools lean on keyword matching and basic filters, which often match people who are superficially aligned but not truly ready to do a deal, while missing non-obvious, high-value connections.

Proof of Match turns the event platform into a driver of trusted, deal-driven relationships for private-market decision makers. It enriches sparse registration data with external signals, scores counterparty fit and readiness through GenAI agents, and ranks matches so every attendee sees a small, prioritized set of conversations worth taking.

02 · Context & vision

Context & vision

At high-intent VC and Web3 events, attendees have a very narrow time window to make the right connections. The default “search + filters + generic keywords” approach produces matches that are technically relevant but not truly deal-ready. An investor might be matched with a founder who fits the thesis and stated intent but is at the wrong fundraising stage or not operationally ready, while founders solving similar problems may never be surfaced together.

The vision for Proof of Match is to be a high-end dealflow engine for small-N rooms, where capital, infrastructure, and policy decisions are made. Instead of maximizing random introductions, it aims to prioritize a short list of thesis-fit, mutually ready connections, backed by richer signals of credibility, stage, and intent.

03 · Users & JTBD

Users & jobs-to-be-done

Investors / allocators

  • Identify a small set of thesis-fit, deal-ready founders, infra providers, and co-investors to add to the pipeline.
  • Understand where capital, regulation, and infrastructure are moving to refine their own theses.

Founders / operators

(startups, infra CEOs)

  • Secure strategic capital and a handful of the right partners (design partners, distribution, infra, regulators).
  • Build a targeted investor/partner map they can nurture after the event.

Regulators / policymakers / public-sector innovation leads

  • Find serious builders and allocators to inform frameworks and potential sandboxes.
  • Map the ecosystem’s systemically important actors and risk areas efficiently.

04 · Pain points

Pain points

P1 – Right counterpart, actually ready

Attendees struggle to find the few people who are both a strong strategic fit and truly ready to do a real deal or pilot in the next cycle, instead of spending time on high-level chats that go nowhere.

P2 – Limited time, multiple intents

Time is extremely limited, and attendees juggle several goals (deploy, raise, find partners, learn). They need help prioritizing a small number of meetings they can turn into long-term relationships, not just more random intros.

P3 – Non-obvious, cross-persona connections

High-value, cross-persona connections (e.g., a regulator or infra partner who shares an underlying problem) are often missed because current tools only match on surface keywords, titles, and simple filters.

05 · Connection signals

Signals that make connections stick

From interviews, fundraising playbooks, and observing how people actually connect in VC and Web3, several signals emerged as critical for whether someone will take a conversation seriously.

  • Thesis fit first – Clear match on sector, stage, geography, and check/token economics so nobody wastes time.
  • Warm intros / shared nodes – Introductions via portfolio founders, mutual connections, or curated circles consistently beat cold outreach and raw profile browsing.
  • Proof of work – Visible shipped product, code, traction, or serious research/regulatory engagement that separates serious actors from tourists and makes everyone lean in.
  • Targeted, informed outreach – Showing you understand the other side’s mandate or constraints (fund thesis, startup runway, regulatory remit) makes the conversation feel like collaboration, not a spray-and-pray pitch.

06 · Architecture

Architecture

Data strategy

  1. Registration as a sparse starting point – The initial form collects basic information: name, company, title, ticket type, self-selected interests, and a free-text field for intent and mandate. From this, the system infers high-level persona archetypes (investor, founder, regulator) and their stated goals.
  2. Enrichment from public sources – To reach deal-ready conversations, the system needs richer signals such as credibility, financial and operational readiness, funding stages, check size, geographic focus, thesis, vertical, and regulatory/compliance tags. Public data sources are used to enrich these signals beyond what attendees type into a form.
  3. Guardrails and human-in-the-loop – Because LLMs can hallucinate and encode bias, any scoring system must use strict, transparent definitions and human review for sensitive attributes. This case study focuses on the high-level scoring and ranking design; operational guardrails are a separate, expert-driven track.

GenAI agent architecture

  1. Data enrichment agents – Extract entities from unstructured sources (sites, profiles, public documents) and output structured fields that extend the registration record.
  2. Embedding & similarity agents – Use embeddings and cosine similarity to capture thematic overlap, primary and inherent secondary intents, and problem-space clustering.
  3. Individual scoring agents – Produce per-attendee scores for credibility, financial, and operational readiness (with the caveat that any such scoring must be carefully defined and audited).
  4. Pair scoring agents – For each potential pair, assess persona fit (investor, founder, regulator), theme and intent overlap, complementary match strength, and non-obvious problem-space ties.
  5. Contextual explainer agents – Translate structured outputs into human-readable “warm intro” blurbs at the UI layer, explaining why two people should meet in the language they use.

07 · Matching & ranking

Matching & ranking logic

Candidate generation buckets

With enriched signals in place, a candidate generation layer classifies potential pairs into priority buckets. A complementary matching bucket captures high-precision matches where persona, thesis, and intents align strongly. A non-obvious bucket captures pairs linked by shared problem space or second-order themes (for example, two founders solving the same underlying infrastructure risk from different angles).

Persona-specific weighting

Matching scores are computed as a weighted sum of component scores: persona fit, intent overlap, thematic fit, complementary strength, non-obviousness, and credibility. Each persona type (investor, founder, regulator) gets its own weight vector so the ranking reflects what they care about most.

For one persona, the matrix can be thought of as “how much this person cares” about each signal, adding up to 100%. For example, an investor might weigh persona fit at 30%, intent overlap at 25%, thematic fit at 20%, complementary score at 15%, non-obviousness at 0%, and credibility at 10%. The same pair will rank differently for a regulator, who might care more about operational readiness and systemic risk than about check size.

Rank score formula

To incorporate both counterparty readiness and self-readiness, the ranking layer uses persona-aware playbook weights for readiness dimensions (e.g., financial vs. operational). At a high level: 

rank_score = match_score × (1 + α × deal_cpty + β × deal_self)

Here, match_score is the weighted sum of component scores for a pair. The coefficients α and β are persona-specific weights for “how much I care about my counterparty’s readiness” and “how much I care about my own readiness”. A persona parameter γ selects which readiness dimension to use (for example, financial vs. operational readiness).

Crucially, this makes the ranking directional: A→B ≠ B→A. An institutional allocator may rank a founder very highly because they are financially and thematically ready, while the regulator persona might rank the same founder lower if operational or risk-related signals are not as strong from their perspective.
